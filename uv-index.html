Sun safety

Smart habits, every day.

The sun does not discriminate. Whether your skin is light, dark, oily, or dry — UV damage is cumulative and silent.

Protection times

When to hide,
when to glide.

UV is strongest between 10am and 4pm. That is when your shadow is shorter than you. If you must be out, find shade, wear a wide hat, and reapply sunscreen before the 2-hour mark.

  • Clouds cut UV by only 20–40% — you still burn through haze
  • Water and sand reflect UV — double exposure near the beach
  • UV penetrates windows — drivers and desk workers near glass need SPF too
Skin cancer awareness

Caught early,
nearly always curable.

Nigeria sees rising cases of skin cancer, especially in albinism and lighter skin. But even dark skin can develop melanoma on the palms, soles, and under nails.

  • Check moles for asymmetry, jagged borders, uneven colour, large diameter, or change — the ABCDE rule
  • Any sore that does not heal in 3 weeks needs a doctor
  • A new dark streak under a fingernail or toenail is a red flag
Clinic advice

What to tell your client.

Sun protection is not a lecture — it is care. Here is how to bring it up in a consultation without sounding alarmist.

Start with their goal

Link SPF to what they want.

If they want anti-ageing, SPF is the only proven prevention. If they want even tone, UV triggers hyperpigmentation. Frame protection as the shortcut to their goal, not a chore.

Skin tone matters

Darker skin needs SPF too.

Melanin gives only ~SPF 13 equivalent. That is not enough. Dark skin is still vulnerable to hyperpigmentation (PIH), melasma, and skin cancer — and UV damage is harder to spot because it does not show as redness.

The reapply talk

One application is not enough.

Most clients apply too little (a shot-glass full for the body, a two-finger length for the face). And they forget to reapply every 2 hours. Show them the quantities in the mirror.

Product pairing

Does it layer well?

Clients worry sunscreen pills under makeup. Recommend lightweight, non-comedogenic formulations for the face, and remind them that powder SPF is a touch-up, not a primary defence.
